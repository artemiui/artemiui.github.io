# the rules and their formal definitions

**rules of inference** are standard logical forms used to deduce valid conclusions from a set of premises .
such rules serve as the fundamental blocks for the construction of formal proofs .

### modus ponens
if $p \supset q$ and $p$ are both true, then $q$ must be true .
-  establish that condition $p$ guarantees result $q$ .
-  confirm that $p$ has indeed occurred .
-  deduce that $q$ is a guaranteed and unavoidable outcome .
-  formula: $(p \supset q), p \vdash q$  

### modus tollens
if $p \supset q$ and $\sim q$ are true, then $\sim p$ is true .
-  set the rule that $p$ strictly requires $q$ to happen .
-  observe that $q$ failed to happen .
-  logically conclude that the initial condition $p$ could not have possibly been met .
-  formula: $(p \supset q), \sim q \vdash \sim p$  
-  *as you may notice, this is precisely why a statement that is true in its antecedent and false in its consequent has a false truth value.* ### disjunctive syllogism
given $p \vee q$ and $\sim p$, you can validly conclude $q$ .
-  recognize a mandatory choice where at least one of $p$ or $q$ must be true .
-  eliminate option $p$ as false .
-  confirm $q$ is the only remaining valid option by process of elimination .
-  formula: $(p \vee q), \sim p \vdash q$  

### hypothetical syllogism
a chain reaction: $p \supset q$ and $q \supset r$ yields $p \supset r$ .
-  link the initial condition $p$ to an intermediate result $q$ .
-  link that intermediate result $q$ directly to a final outcome $r$ .
-  trace the transitive path to bridge the first premise $p$ directly to the final outcome $r$ .
-  formula: $(p \supset q), (q \supset r) \vdash p \supset r$  

### constructive dilemma
given $(p \supset q) \cdot (r \supset s)$ and the disjunction $p \vee r$, the result is $q \vee s$ .
-  establish two separate conditional pathways where $p$ leads to $q$, and $r$ leads to $s$ .
-  confirm that at least one of the starting points, $p$ or $r$, is definitively true .
-  safely deduce that at least one of their respective destinations, $q$ or $s$, will logically follow .
-  formula: $(p \supset q) \cdot (r \supset s), (p \vee r) \vdash q \vee s$  

### destructive dilemma
given $(p \supset q) \cdot (r \supset s)$ and $\sim q \vee \sim s$, you deduce $\sim p \vee \sim r$ .
-  map out two conditional rules where $p$ leads to $q$ and $r$ leads to $s$ .
-  observe that at least one of the expected results, $q$ or $s$, failed to occur .
-  trace backward to deduce that at least one of the initial triggers, $p$ or $r$, must have been false to begin with .
-  formula: $(p \supset q) \cdot (r \supset s), (\sim q \vee \sim s) \vdash \sim p \vee \sim r$  

### conjunction
two separate premises $p$ and $q$ can be joined together as $p \cdot q$ .
-  identify a verified truth $p$ .
-  identify another verified truth $q$ .
-  bind them together, as both are simultaneously true independent of one another .
-  formula: $p, q \vdash p \cdot q$  

### simplification
from a conjunction $p \cdot q$, you can independently extract $p$ (or $q$) .
-  introduce a compound statement where both $p$ and $q$ are verified together .
-  isolate one component .
-  conclude it is independently true, because if both are true concurrently, each remains true when evaluated individually .
-  formula: $(p \cdot q) \vdash p$  

### addition
if premise $p$ is true, you can add any proposition to form $p \vee q$ .
-  confirm a solid truth $p$ .
-  construct a disjunction combining $p$ with an unverified unknown $q$ .
-  recognize this is fully valid because a disjunctive statement only requires a minimum of one true component, which $p$ naturally satisfies .
-  formula: $p \vdash p \vee q$  

---

**rules of replacement** (or logical equivalences) differ from rules of inference because they are bi-directional . they state that two propositions have the exact same truth value in every possible scenario, meaning you can substitute one for the other anywhere in a proof . we use the symbol $\equiv$ to denote this strict equivalence .

### de morgan's theorems (DeM)
the negation of a conjunction is the disjunction of the negations, and vice versa .
-  distribute the negation across the terms inside the parentheses .
-  flip the operator from a conjunction to a disjunction (or disjunction to conjunction) .
-  formula: $\sim (p \cdot q) \equiv (\sim p \vee \sim q)$ and $\sim (p \vee q) \equiv (\sim p \cdot \sim q)$

### commutativity (Com)
the order of the terms in a conjunction or disjunction does not matter .
-  swap the positions of the two propositions .
-  recognize that the overall truth value remains completely unchanged .
-  formula: $(p \vee q) \equiv (q \vee p)$ and $(p \cdot q) \equiv (q \cdot p)$

### associativity (Assoc)
the grouping of three or more identical consecutive operators does not matter .
-  shift the parentheses to group different terms together .
-  formula: $[p \vee (q \vee r)] \equiv [(p \vee q) \vee r]$ and $[p \cdot (q \cdot r)] \equiv [(p \cdot q) \cdot r]$

### distribution (Dist)
a proposition conjoined or disjoined to a parenthetical statement can be distributed to each term inside .
-  apply the outside term and its operator to each inside term .
-  join the newly formed groupings with the operator that was originally inside the parentheses .
-  formula: $[p \cdot (q \vee r)] \equiv [(p \cdot q) \vee (p \cdot r)]$ and $[p \vee (q \cdot r)] \equiv [(p \vee q) \cdot (p \vee r)]$

### double negation (DN)
the negation of a negated proposition is equivalent to the original affirmative proposition .
-  remove or add two consecutive negation signs without changing the truth value .
-  formula: $p \equiv \sim \sim p$

### transposition (Trans)
a conditional statement is equivalent to its contrapositive .
-  swap the antecedent and the consequent .
-  negate both of the new terms .
-  formula: $(p \supset q) \equiv (\sim q \supset \sim p)$

### material implication (Impl)
a conditional statement is logically equivalent to a disjunction where the antecedent is negated .
-  change the implication to a disjunction .
-  negate the first term (the antecedent) while leaving the second term (the consequent) as is .
-  formula: $(p \supset q) \equiv (\sim p \vee q)$

### material equivalence (Equiv)
a biconditional statement means both propositions imply each other, or both share the exact same truth value .
-  expand the biconditional into two separate conditional statements joined by a conjunction .
-  alternatively, express it as the disjunction of them both being true or both being false .
-  formula: $(p \equiv q) \equiv [(p \supset q) \cdot (q \supset p)]$ and $(p \equiv q) \equiv [(p \cdot q) \vee (\sim p \cdot \sim q)]$

### exportation (Exp)
if a conditional statement has a conjunction as its antecedent, it can be separated into a chain of conditionals .
-  extract one of the conjoined terms from the antecedent .
-  turn it into the antecedent of a new, nested conditional statement .
-  formula: $[(p \cdot q) \supset r] \equiv [p \supset (q \supset r)]$

### tautology (Taut)
a proposition conjoined or disjoined with itself is logically equivalent to itself .
-  simplify redundant identical terms .
-  formula: $p \equiv (p \vee p)$ and $p \equiv (p \cdot p)$

# how do these work in natural language?

here i provide examples in everyday language and how each rule would translate accordingly .

### modus ponens
- $P_1$: if it is raining, then the street is wet. ($p \supset q$)
- $P_2$: it is raining. ($p$)
- $C$: therefore, the street is wet. ($\therefore q$)  

### modus tollens
- $P_1$: if my pomodoro cycle ends, the timer will ring. ($p \supset q$)  
- $P_2$: the timer did not ring. ($\sim q$)  
- $C$: therefore, my pomodoro cycle has not ended yet. ($\therefore \sim p$)  

### disjunctive syllogism
- $P_1$: i will drink coffee or i will drink tea. ($p \vee q$)  
- $P_2$: i will not drink tea. ($\sim p$)  
- $C$: therefore, i will drink coffee. ($\therefore q$)  

### hypothetical syllogism
- $P_1$: if i study hard, i will pass the exam. ($p \supset q$)  
- $P_2$: if i pass the exam, i will graduate. ($q \supset r$)  
- $C$: therefore, if i study hard, i will graduate. ($\therefore p \supset r$)  

### constructive dilemma
- $P_1$: if i go to the movies, i will spend money; and if i stay home, i will be bored. ($(p \supset q)  \cdot (r \supset s)$) 
- $P_2$: either i go to the movies or stay home. ($p \vee r$)  
- $C$: therefore, i will either spend money or be bored. ($\therefore q \vee s$)  

### destructive dilemma
- $P_1$: if it is a bird, it has wings; and if it is a fish, it has gills. ($(p \supset q)  \cdot (r \supset s)$) 
- $P_2$: the animal either does not have wings or it does not have gills. ($\sim q \vee \sim s$)  
- $C$: therefore, the animal is either not a bird or not a fish. ($\therefore \sim p \vee \sim r$)  

### conjunction
- $P_1$: the sun is shining. ($p$)  
- $P_2$: the birds are singing. ($q$)  
- $C$: therefore, the sun is shining and the birds are singing. ($\therefore p \cdot q$)  

### simplification
- $P_1$: the book is long and the book is difficult. ($p \cdot q$)  
- $C$: therefore, the book is long. ($\therefore p$)  

### addition
- $P_1$: i have an apple. ($p$)  
- $C$: therefore, i have an apple or i have a banana. ($\therefore p \vee q$)

### de morgan's theorems
- $P_1$: it is not the case that i am both tired and hungry. ($\sim (p \cdot q)$)
- $C$: therefore, either i am not tired, or i am not hungry. ($\therefore \sim p \vee \sim q$)

### commutativity
- $P_1$: i have a cat and i have a dog. ($p \cdot q$)
- $C$: therefore, i have a dog and i have a cat. ($\therefore q \cdot p$)

### associativity
- $P_1$: i will buy apples, and also bananas and cherries. ($p \cdot (q \cdot r)$)
- $C$: therefore, i will buy apples and bananas, and also cherries. ($\therefore (p \cdot q) \cdot r$)

### distribution
- $P_1$: i will have coffee, and either a pastry or a sandwich. ($p \cdot (q \vee r)$)
- $C$: therefore, i will either have coffee and a pastry, or i will have coffee and a sandwich. ($\therefore (p \cdot q) \vee (p \cdot r)$)

### double negation
- $P_1$: it is not true that i am not going. ($\sim \sim p$)
- $C$: therefore, i am going. ($\therefore p$)

### transposition
- $P_1$: if it is a dog, then it is a mammal. ($p \supset q$)
- $C$: therefore, if it is not a mammal, then it is not a dog. ($\therefore \sim q \supset \sim p$)

### material implication
- $P_1$: if you study, you will pass. ($p \supset q$)
- $C$: therefore, either you do not study, or you will pass. ($\therefore \sim p \vee q$)

### material equivalence
- $P_1$: we will play the game if and only if it is sunny. ($p \equiv q$)
- $C$: therefore, if we play the game then it is sunny, and if it is sunny then we will play the game. ($\therefore (p \supset q) \cdot (q \supset p)$)

### exportation
- $P_1$: if you have a ticket and you are in line, you can enter. ($(p \cdot q) \supset r$)
- $C$: therefore, if you have a ticket, then if you are in line, you can enter. ($\therefore p \supset (q \supset r)$)

### tautology
- $P_1$: the sun is shining. ($p$)
- $C$: therefore, the sun is shining or the sun is shining. ($\therefore p \vee p$)
